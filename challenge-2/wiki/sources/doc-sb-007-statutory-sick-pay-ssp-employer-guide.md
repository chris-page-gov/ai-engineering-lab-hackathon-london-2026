## Current SSP rates and qualifying days

SSP is paid for qualifying days only. Qualifying days are the days on which the employee would normally be required to work under their contract of employment.

| Tax year | Weekly rate | Daily rate (5 qualifying days) | Daily rate (6 qualifying days) |
|----|----|----|----|
| 2025-26 | £116.75 | £23.35 | £19.46 |
| 2024-25 | £109.40 | £21.88 | £18.23 |

SSP rates from April 2025 {.govuk-table}

SSP is payable for up to 28 weeks in a single period of incapacity for work. The first 3 qualifying days in any period of incapacity are "waiting days" during which SSP is not payable.

</div>

<div id="qualifying-conditions" class="section">

## Qualifying conditions

An employee qualifies for SSP if all of the following conditions are met:

- They are classed as an employee and have done some work for you
- They have been off work sick for 4 or more consecutive days (including non-working days)
- They earn an average of at least £123 per week (the Lower Earnings Limit for 2025-26)
- They have notified you of their sickness within the required deadline

</div>

<div id="employee-duties" class="section">

## Employee duties

Employees are responsible for notifying their employer of their incapacity in a timely manner. **Employees must notify their employer within 7 days of the first day of incapacity.** If the employer has an agreed notification procedure (for example, telephoning a specific number by a particular time), the employee should follow that procedure where reasonably practicable.

For absences lasting more than 7 days, the employee should provide a "fit note" (Statement of Fitness for Work) from a doctor, registered nurse, pharmacist, physiotherapist, or occupational therapist. For absences of 7 days or fewer, the employer may accept self-certification.

Where the employee is also claiming other state benefits, they should inform both their employer and the relevant benefit office of the period of sickness. This is particularly important for employees receiving Housing Benefit, as periods of sickness absence may affect their income-based benefit calculations (see [DOC-HB-001: Housing Benefit eligibility and rates](doc-hb-001-housing-benefit-check-if-you-re-eligible-gov-uk.md)).

</div>

<div id="employer-obligations" class="section">

## Employer obligations

Employers must pay SSP to qualifying employees from the fourth qualifying day of sickness absence. Payment should be made on the employee's normal payday and is subject to tax and National Insurance deductions in the usual way.

Employers should note that employees have **up to 28 days to provide notification of incapacity**, after which SSP may be withheld. Employers must not withhold SSP on the grounds of late notification if the employee has provided notice within this period, unless the employer has a contractual notification procedure and the employee has unreasonably failed to follow it.

If an employer decides to withhold SSP, they must issue the employee with form SSP1 explaining the reasons. The employee can then apply to HMRC for a formal decision.

</div>

<div id="record-keeping" class="section">

## Record-keeping

Employers are no longer required to keep specific SSP records (the previous requirement to maintain form SSP2 was abolished in 2014). However, HMRC strongly recommends that employers maintain records of:

- Dates of each employee's period of incapacity for work
- The amount of SSP paid and the dates of payment
- Any dates within a period of incapacity that SSP was not paid and the reasons
- Fit notes and self-certification documents received

These records should be retained for at least 3 years after the end of the tax year to which they relate.

</div>

<div id="occupational-sick-pay" class="section">

## Interaction with occupational sick pay

Many employers operate their own occupational sick pay (OSP) schemes that provide more generous payments than SSP. Where an employer pays OSP that equals or exceeds the SSP rate, the employer is treated as having paid SSP and has no further SSP liability for that period.

If the OSP scheme pays less than the SSP rate, the employer must "top up" the payment to at least the SSP rate for the duration of the SSP entitlement period. The OSP and SSP amounts should be clearly identified on the employee's payslip.

</div>

<div id="benefits-interaction" class="section">

## What if your employee is also claiming benefits?

Employees who are receiving Statutory Sick Pay may also be entitled to means-tested benefits such as Housing Benefit or Council Tax Reduction. SSP counts as earned income for the purposes of calculating these benefits. The local authority will take the employee's SSP into account when assessing their Housing Benefit entitlement under the standard means test. For further details on how income from employment affects Housing Benefit, see [DOC-HB-001: Housing Benefit eligibility and rates](doc-hb-001-housing-benefit-check-if-you-re-eligible-gov-uk.md).

Employers are not required to notify the local authority that an employee is receiving SSP, but should be prepared to provide confirmation of SSP payments if requested by the employee or the local authority for benefit assessment purposes.

</div>
