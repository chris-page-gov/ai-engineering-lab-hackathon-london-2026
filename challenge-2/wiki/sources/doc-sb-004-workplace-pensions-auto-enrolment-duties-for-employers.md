WORKPLACE PENSIONS: AUTO-ENROLMENT DUTIES FOR EMPLOYERS
========================================================
Document Reference: DOC-SB-004
Status: Current
Published: 2025-06-15
Last Updated: 2025-11-20

------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. INTRODUCTION

1.1 Overview of auto-enrolment

    All employers in the United Kingdom are required by law to enrol
    eligible workers into a qualifying workplace pension scheme and
    make contributions to that scheme. This duty applies regardless of
    the size of the employer. The obligations were introduced under the
    Pensions Act 2008 and have been phased in since 2012 through a
    series of staging dates allocated to employers based on the size of
    their PAYE scheme.

1.2 Who this guidance is for

    This guidance is intended for employers, payroll administrators,
    and business advisers. It explains the legal duties, the process
    for enrolling workers, the minimum contribution rates, opt-out
    procedures, and the consequences of non-compliance.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

2. EMPLOYER DUTIES

2.1 Assessing your workforce

    2.1.1 Categories of worker

        You must assess each person who works for you on the first day
        of their employment and on every subsequent pay reference period.
        Workers fall into one of three categories:

        2.1.1.1 Eligible jobholders

            Workers aged between 22 and State Pension age who earn more
            than 10,000 pounds per year (or the equivalent pro-rated
            amount per pay period). You must automatically enrol these
            workers into your pension scheme.

        2.1.1.2 Non-eligible jobholders

            Workers aged between 16 and 74 who earn more than the lower
            earnings threshold of 6,240 pounds per year but do not meet
            the full criteria for eligible jobholders (for example,
            workers aged 16 to 21 or those earning between 6,240 and
            10,000 pounds). These workers have the right to opt in.

        2.1.1.3 Entitled workers

            Workers aged between 16 and 74 who earn below 6,240 pounds
            per year. These workers have the right to join a pension
            scheme, but you are not required to make employer
            contributions for them.

    2.1.2 Postponement

        You may choose to postpone automatic enrolment for up to three
        months from either your staging date or the date a new employee
        joins. You must issue a postponement notice to the worker within
        six weeks of the start of the postponement period.

2.2 Choosing a pension scheme

    2.2.1 Qualifying schemes

        The pension scheme you use must meet certain criteria. For a
        defined contribution scheme, total contributions must be at
        least the statutory minimum percentage of qualifying earnings.
        For a defined benefit scheme, the scheme must provide benefits
        that meet or exceed the test scheme standard.

    2.2.2 NEST as a default option

        If you do not have an existing pension scheme, you can use the
        National Employment Savings Trust (NEST), which was set up by
        the government to ensure all employers have access to a low-cost
        qualifying scheme. There is no charge to the employer for using
        NEST, though the scheme applies a contribution charge to members.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

3. CONTRIBUTION RATES

3.1 Minimum contribution rates

    The minimum contributions for defined contribution schemes are
    calculated as a percentage of qualifying earnings. Qualifying
    earnings are the portion of a worker's pay between the lower
    earnings threshold and the upper earnings threshold (currently
    6,240 pounds and 50,270 pounds respectively).

    Date Range          | Employer Min | Employee Min | Total Min
    --------------------|--------------|--------------|----------
    Before Apr 2018     | 1%           | 1%           | 2%
    Apr 2018 - Mar 2019 | 2%           | 3%           | 5%
    Apr 2019 onwards    | 3%           | 5%           | 8%

3.2 Certification

    Employers who wish to use an alternative definition of pensionable
    pay (for example, basic pay rather than qualifying earnings) may
    do so provided they self-certify that total contributions across
    their workforce will meet or exceed the statutory minimum. This
    certification must be reviewed and re-issued every 18 months.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

4. OPT-OUT PROCEDURES

4.1 Worker's right to opt out

    4.1.1 Opt-out window

        A worker who has been automatically enrolled may choose to opt
        out within one calendar month of the date they were enrolled
        (or the date they received their enrolment information, if
        later). If a worker opts out within this window, any
        contributions already deducted must be refunded in full.

    4.1.2 Ceasing active membership after the opt-out window

        After the one-month opt-out window has closed, a worker may
        still choose to cease active membership of the scheme. However,
        contributions already paid will not be refunded and will remain
        in the pension pot.

4.2 Re-enrolment

    4.2.1 Cyclical re-enrolment

        Approximately every three years from your staging date, you must
        re-enrol any eligible jobholders who previously opted out or
        ceased active membership. This is known as cyclical re-enrolment.
        You have a six-month window around your re-enrolment date to
        complete this process.

    4.2.2 Inducement prohibition

        You must not induce, coerce, or attempt to persuade a worker to
        opt out of their pension. Doing so is a criminal offence. This
        includes offering financial incentives to opt out, making opt-out
        a condition of employment, or treating workers unfavourably
        because they remain in the scheme.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

5. COMPLIANCE AND PENALTIES

5.1 The Pensions Regulator's powers

    The Pensions Regulator (TPR) is responsible for ensuring employers
    comply with their auto-enrolment duties. TPR has a range of
    enforcement tools available, escalating in severity.

5.2 Penalty structure

    5.2.1 Compliance notice

        TPR may issue a compliance notice requiring you to take specific
        actions (such as enrolling workers or paying contributions) by
        a stated deadline.

    5.2.2 Fixed penalty notice

        If you do not comply with a compliance notice, TPR may issue a
        fixed penalty notice of 400 pounds.

    5.2.3 Escalating penalty notice

        If non-compliance continues after a fixed penalty notice, TPR
        may issue an escalating daily penalty. The daily rate depends
        on the number of workers in your PAYE scheme:

        Number of workers    | Daily penalty
        ---------------------|-------------
        1 to 4               | 50 pounds
        5 to 49              | 500 pounds
        50 to 249            | 2,500 pounds
        250 to 499           | 5,000 pounds
        500 or more          | 10,000 pounds

    5.2.4 Criminal prosecution

        In the most serious cases, particularly where an employer
        wilfully fails to comply or induces workers to opt out, TPR
        may pursue criminal prosecution.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

6. RECORD-KEEPING

    You must keep records of your auto-enrolment activities for six
    years. This includes the names and addresses of enrolled workers,
    the dates of enrolment and any opt-outs, contribution amounts paid,
    and the pension scheme reference numbers. Failure to keep adequate
    records may itself result in a compliance notice from TPR.
