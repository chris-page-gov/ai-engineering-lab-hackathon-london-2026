         Equality Impact Assessment Universal Credit
                    Migration Programme
Date: September 2023 | SRO: [REDACTED] | Lead analyst: [REDACTED]


Executive summary

This assessment considers the potential equality impacts of the managed migration of legacy benefit
claimants to Universal Credit. It covers all protected characteristics under the Equality Act 2010.

The assessment finds that migration will have differential impacts on certain groups, particularly:

      • Disabled claimants (who may experience disruption to existing support arrangements)

      • Claimants over State Pension age (who will not migrate but may be affected indirectly)

      • Women (who are more likely to be affected by changes to childcare element calculations)

      • Ethnic minority claimants (who may face additional barriers to digital engagement)



1. Background

The Department is migrating claimants from legacy benefits (Income Support, income-based Jobseeker’s
Allowance, income-related Employment and Support Allowance, Housing Benefit, Child Tax Credit, and
Working Tax Credit) to Universal Credit. This programme is known as "Move to UC" and is expected to be
completed by March 2025.

      [NOTE: The March 2025 completion date was the target at the time of writing. The programme has
      since been extended. Check the latest published timeline.]



2. Protected characteristics analysis

2.1 Age

Claimants of State Pension age claiming Housing Benefit only will not be migrated. This means they retain
their existing benefit arrangements.

Younger claimants (under 25) receive a lower personal allowance under UC than under legacy benefits in
some circumstances.

Impact: Mixed. Younger claimants may receive less; older claimants are protected from disruption.


2.2 Disability

Disabled claimants on ESA who are in the Support Group will transition to the UC equivalent (LCWRA —
Limited Capability for Work and Work-Related Activity). Transitional protection ensures they will not
receive less in cash terms at the point of migration.
However:

      • The LCWRA element in UC (£390.06/month) is lower than the ESA Support Group component
         plus the disability premium available in legacy benefits

      • Transitional protection is eroded over time as UC rates are uprated but the transitional element is
         not

      • The work capability assessment process for UC differs from ESA, which may cause anxiety


2.3 Sex

Women are disproportionately affected by:

      • Changes to childcare support calculation methodology

      • The two-child limit (applicable to UC but not to legacy Child Tax Credit claims made before April
         2017)

      • The move to monthly assessment periods, which can create budgeting difficulties for those
         managing household finances


2.4 Race and ethnicity

Research indicates that claimants from some ethnic minority backgrounds may face additional barriers:

      • Digital literacy and access to online services

      • Language barriers in engaging with UC’s online journal

      • Cultural factors affecting engagement with work coaches

The Department provides translation services and face-to-face support, but take-up of these services is
uneven.
