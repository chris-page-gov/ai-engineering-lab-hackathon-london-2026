# Right to request flexible working: frequently asked questions

This document answers common questions about the statutory right to request flexible working. The right was significantly expanded by the Employment Relations (Flexible Working) Act 2023, which came into force on 6 April 2024.

---

**Q1. Who has the right to request flexible working?**

All employees have the right to request flexible working from the first day of their employment. The previous requirement to have 26 weeks of continuous service was removed on 6 April 2024. Agency workers, contractors, and other non-employees do not have the statutory right, but may be able to make informal requests.

---

**Q2. What types of flexible working can I request?**

You can request any change to the terms and conditions of your employment that relates to:

- The hours you are required to work
- The times when you are required to work
- The place where you are required to work (including working from home)
- Any combination of the above

Common types of flexible working include part-time working, compressed hours, flexitime, annualised hours, staggered hours, job sharing, and remote or hybrid working.

---

**Q3. How many requests can I make?**

Employees may make up to 2 statutory requests in any 12-month period. Each request must be made in writing, must be dated, and must state that it is a statutory request made under section 80F of the Employment Rights Act 1996.

---

**Q4. Does my employer have to agree to my request?**

No. The right is a right to request, not a right to have. However, your employer must deal with your request in a reasonable manner and must make a decision within 2 months of receiving the request (unless you agree to a longer period). An employer may only refuse a request on one or more of the 8 statutory grounds set out in the legislation.

---

**Q5. What are the statutory grounds for refusal?**

An employer may refuse a flexible working request only on one or more of the following grounds:

- The burden of additional costs
- A detrimental effect on ability to meet customer demand
- An inability to reorganise work among existing staff
- An inability to recruit additional staff
- A detrimental impact on quality
- A detrimental impact on performance
- Insufficiency of work during the periods the employee proposes to work
- Planned structural changes

The employer must consult with the employee before refusing a request. This consultation requirement was introduced by The Flexible Working Regulations 2014 (SI 2014/1398) and strengthened by the 2023 Act.

---

**Q6. What happens if my employer does not follow the correct process?**

If your employer fails to deal with your request in a reasonable manner, fails to notify you of their decision within the statutory time limit, or rejects your request on grounds other than the 8 permitted statutory reasons, you may bring a complaint to an employment tribunal. The tribunal may order the employer to reconsider the request and may award compensation of up to 8 weeks' pay.

---

**Q7. Can my employer dismiss me for making a flexible working request?**

Dismissing an employee because they made a flexible working request would normally be treated as automatic unfair dismissal. Similarly, subjecting an employee to any detriment (such as denial of promotion, reduction in hours, or exclusion from projects) because they made a request is unlawful. You are protected from the point at which you submit the request.

---

**Q8. I am a carer. Do I have any additional rights?**

The Carer's Leave Act 2023 gives employees who are carers the right to take up to one week of unpaid leave per year for caring purposes. While this is separate from flexible working, employees who have caring responsibilities may find it helpful to combine a flexible working request with their entitlement to carer's leave. Employers should consider caring responsibilities sympathetically when assessing flexible working requests.

---

**Q9. Can I withdraw my request?**

Yes. You may withdraw a statutory flexible working request at any time before the employer has made a decision. If you withdraw your request, it still counts as one of your 2 permitted requests in the 12-month period. You should withdraw the request in writing and keep a copy for your records.

---

**Q10. My employer has agreed to my request. Can they change their mind later?**

Once an employer has agreed to a flexible working request, the change becomes a permanent variation to the employee's contract of employment, unless both parties have agreed that the change will be for a trial period or a fixed duration. The employer cannot unilaterally reverse the change without the employee's agreement. Any attempt to do so may amount to a breach of contract or constructive dismissal. For wider information on employer duties, see [DOC-SB-005: Employer obligations overview](doc-sb-005-employment-rights-act-1996-plain-english-summary.md).
