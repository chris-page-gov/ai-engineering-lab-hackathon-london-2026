1. What is data protection?

Data protection law governs how personal data is collected, stored, used, and shared. The UK General
Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) and the Data Protection Act 2018 set out the legal framework. All
staff who handle personal data must understand and comply with these requirements.

Personal data means any information relating to an identified or identifiable living individual. This includes
obvious identifiers like names and National Insurance numbers, but also less obvious ones like IP
addresses, location data, and online identifiers.

Special category data — including information about health, ethnicity, political opinions, trade union
membership, and biometric data — has additional protections and must only be processed where a
specific legal basis applies.



2. Your responsibilities

Every member of staff is responsible for:

      • Only accessing personal data you need for your work

      • Not sharing personal data with anyone who does not need it

      • Keeping personal data secure (locked screens, encrypted devices, secure email)

      • Reporting any suspected data breach immediately

      • Completing mandatory data protection training annually

Failure to comply with data protection requirements is a disciplinary matter and may also be a criminal
offence under the Data Protection Act 2018.
3. Handling personal data

3.1 Collection

Only collect personal data that is necessary for the specific purpose. Do not collect data "just in case" it
might be useful.


3.2 Storage

Personal data must be stored on approved departmental systems only. It must not be stored on personal
devices, personal email accounts, or unapproved cloud services.

Retention periods vary by data type. See the departmental retention schedule (available on the intranet)
for specific guidance.


3.3 Sharing

Before sharing personal data outside the department, you must:

      (a) Confirm there is a lawful basis for sharing

      (b) Check whether a data sharing agreement is in place

      (c) Use secure transfer methods

      (d) Record what was shared and with whom


3.4 Disposal

Personal data must be disposed of securely when no longer needed. Paper records should be placed in
confidential waste bins. Electronic records should be deleted in accordance with the retention schedule.



4. Data breaches

A personal data breach is any event that leads to the accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration,
unauthorised disclosure of, or access to, personal data.

Examples:

      • Sending an email containing personal data to the wrong recipient

      • Losing an unencrypted laptop or USB stick

      • Leaving paper records in an unsecured location

      • A cyber attack that exposes personal data

All breaches must be reported to the Data Protection Officer within 24 hours using the breach reporting
form on the intranet. Serious breaches must be reported to the Information Commissioner’s Office within
72 hours — the DPO will handle this.
5. Subject access requests

Individuals have the right to request a copy of the personal data we hold about them. These requests are
known as Subject Access Requests (SARs).

If you receive a SAR, forward it immediately to the SAR team at sar@dwp.gov.uk. Do not attempt to
respond directly.

The department has 30 calendar days to respond to a SAR from receipt.
